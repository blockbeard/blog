# Blog Envy

My [Daggerheart GM](https://blog.arrowed.games/how-i-daggerheart/) and one of my [fellow players](https://patchworkpaladin.com/2026/03/13/the-daggerheart-meatgrinder/) have both blogged about our game and they both made me want to write a post of my own that I never quite got around to. 
Since then we have finished the first arc of our campaign, thirty-three sessions progressing from level 1 to level 6 and are taking a hiatus to play in a different campaign frame while one player takes some time off. 
This post will be mostly a review and discussion of why I think it supports my favourite playstyle coloured by my experience playing a decent-length campaign. For a more in-depth review I suggest Valeria Loves' wonderful [Dishonest Review (full of lies)](https://valerialoves.com/daggerheart-a-dishonest-review-full-of-lies/).

## The Book
Gorgeous. Well laid out, nice art and plenty of it with a mixture of typical heroic stuff and a good selection of cutesy/creepy weirdos with, to my (GenX, cishet dude) eye, a good spread of representation of gender, race and culture. 

![Two-page spread from the Sorcerer class chapter (pages 46–47). The left page lists the class's domains, hit points, hope feature and class features alongside an illustration of a dark-haired sorceress with crackling energy at her fingertips. The right page covers the Elemental Origin and Primal Origin subclasses with their features, plus background and connection questions, illustrated by a frog-like figure riding a fish through a watery scene](daggerheart-sorcerer-spread.webp)

It has a good amount of art in a few different styles, there is the very polished character art but also lots of nice line drawings. Most of the pages have only the faintest background texture that makes it feel very clean and open. It is a delight to read.

![Two-page spread from the Ancestry chapter (pages 66–67) showing the Infernis and Katari ancestries. Each page has a block of descriptive text and ancestry features alongside a full-colour character portrait — a purple-skinned, horned Infernis on the left and a red-haired, leonine Katari archer on the right — surrounded by pencil concept sketches showing variations in horns, tails, faces, paws and body types.](daggerheart-ancestry-spread.webp)

Physically it is a good quality sewn hardback, double ribboned, nice endpapers and cheap. Not Cheap-cheap, it's still $60/£60 (what's that about Darrington? my calculator says $60 is £45) but considering you get a full set of cards and the pdf if you buy direct or from a partner retailer, well the last book WotC did with cards and a box was $110 for a book half the size and if you wanted an electronic copy you were in for another $30 so I think the value is exceptional. 

## The System
It is good and I like almost all of it. The (lack of) initiative system, damage thresholds and Hope and Fear are all easier to do at the table than they are to explain and in thirty-three sessions rules lookups were mostly checking ability wordings to make sure they did what we thought they did or checking exactly how death moves worked to make sure we fully understood the consequences. 
I'm not going to reiterate all of the core mechanics, if you want all the details you can go read the [free SRD](https://www.daggerheart.com/srd/) but there are a few stand-outs.  

Characters are crunchy with abilities from their class, subclass, ancestry and community. 
Rather than skills you have experiences that you can add to your rolls by spending a Hope. I find this really satisfying. Instead of a whole list of narrow skills I can take "court wizard" and by spending a Hope add to any roll that I can justify it for. Reading runes? Navigating a social situation? Searching a library? They all work. It seems open to abuse until you remember that you need to spend the Hope. You could take "good at everything" as an experience and it wouldn't break the game. 
Levelling up is a genuine joy, not just because of the power but because we made meaningful choices about how our characters grew in response to what had happened in the fiction, instead of set abilities you get to pick from a menu of options and can build the same class into very different characters. There is something here for optimisers, but the game doesn't demand it and the rewards for it are small. 
The character options are all on cards so you can shuffle them and draw a random character, which is how I generated mine, and they seem to work just fine. I don't think by the end anyone would have suspected my orc druid of having been a random pick. 

The system is asymmetrical. There is no pretence that the GM and players are the same. There are a few differences between how PCs and adversaries play but the big difference is that players roll 2d12 with bell-curve results that drive the metacurrency and crit 1-in-12. The GM rolls a d20 which is swingier and only crits 1-in-20.
I like it. There is no reason to make everyone's mechanics the same when only one of the people at the table can decide the next room has a giant in it. 

If I had one criticism it would be that it reads as crunchy and number heavy but plays pretty loose with range bands and gold that comes in handfuls, bags and chests and a fair number of things that need GM judgement calls. But it works, the annoying things like not being able to attack because you were 5 feet too far away, or figuring out how much copper an ale is when I just robbed a dragon have never been the parts that I thought made for fun games.  

## The Playstyle
My [[How to win a TTRPG|last post]] was about how I think that "you surround yourself with talented people you love, and who love you, give them an idea with enough empty space to invite them in and make it their own... and when it returns to you, it's more beautiful than you imagined." (Jim Henson on making art) was the perfect philosophy when it came to RPG play. 

Daggerheart goes out of its way to encourage this. Talent of course is subjective and I redefined it as being open to the rest of the proposition, perhaps it could also be said to include playing with people whose style fits with yours and I can see how this is not a game for everyone. If you are uncomfortable with abstraction, think the GM should do all of the world-building and wouldn't like a "stick them with the pointy end" experience because it doesn't specify exactly what it does then it may not be for you. 

Love might be better defined as trust and there is a definite need for trust here, the game emphasises collaborative world-building and I can see that going badly with players who want to be disruptive or struggle with being put on the spot, but it starts you off slowly and builds trust as you go. 
There are a set of connection questions you can ask the other characters and they make you just vulnerable enough to be slightly uncomfortable. Things like "Why do you act differently when we’re alone than when others are around?" it's a nice way to put yourself out there a little but still be in the framework of the game. As well as building some trust the questions are great for bringing the characters together rather than just being a group of people who met in a tavern. I do think this is a game that benefits from love or trust between the players (and I include the GM in that) it isn't built with strict rules designed to keep the assholes in check. It trusts you to behave like adults rather than trying to enforce it. 

There is lots in the book for the GM to be able to invite you in, give you space and make it your own. From starting with a session zero with concrete advice on what to do, not just safety stuff, but CATS (concept, aim, tone, subject - not the Zero Wing guy) and world-building to establish the cooperative dynamic early but there are also examples of the kinds of world-building questions you might want to ask the players all through the book. 

![Pixel-art of the Zero Wing villain CATS captioned "CATS: HOW ARE YOU GENTLEMEN" — a green-skinned cyborg in a purple cape grinning against a black background.](/published_assets/cats-how-are-you.webp)

In our campaign I certainly felt that we built something more beautiful than any of us imagined when we started. 

## The Campaign

The five of us played thirty-three sessions plus a session zero. We went from levels 1 to 6, had three character deaths, two permanent and all meaningful from at least twice that many death moves. 
The first one was our faun ranger, choked by a mushroom creature, they rolled the dice on a Risk It All (roll your Duality Dice, back to one health on a roll with Hope, die on a roll with Fear) and failed. The second a Blaze of Glory (one last turn with a guaranteed crit, but then die) from our goblin seraph, they spent a while as a ghost before being resurrected and the third from our giant rogue, depressed after taking scars from previous death moves who tried another Risk It All that didn't work out.

We may just have a sadistic GM, but the ability to choose not to die and just be unconscious for the rest of the scene with a chance of taking a scar (losing a Hope slot) did not make our game less deadly. The fact that players have more control over their own fate seems to make it more deadly as the GM doesn't have to hold back. 

We took a blank map of the archipelago and filled it out as we went. We ended up with a race of guardian giants petrified and mostly buried, colloquially known as "big skullians", due to there being a couple of map features that looked kind of like big skulls, visited a haunted temple in the desert because it looked a bit like a spa, invented a rainbow pantheon of gods inspired by our colourblind seraph, and countless details and stories of the places we visited and people we met that we came up with together. 

I won't say it is the best game I have ever played in (see my soon-to-be-written Dolmenwood review) but it was up there, and it was certainly the game I felt most involved in crafting the world for as a player. In fact I'm not sure I've ever done so much to shape the world when I've been the GM as I mostly play in published settings. 

## Conclusion
I think this is the best high fantasy game I've played, the system is crunchy enough to be satisfying and provide some of the solo play fun of planning out your character but free enough that rulings over rules and a looser playstyle seems completely natural. 
It probably isn't for everyone, but I expect you can figure out whether or not it is for you by looking at the cover with its blend of heroic archetypes and a cutesy frog guy. 

It is certainly for me. I don't know or really care whether it will be "The D&D killer". I expect not considering the other game's size and legacy, but I certainly can't see myself going back unless they do some enormous missteps in the expansions they have announced.
