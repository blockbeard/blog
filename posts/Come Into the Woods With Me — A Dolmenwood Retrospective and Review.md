I recently had the great pleasure of playing in my friend Patchwork Paladin's [Dolmenwood](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/528570/dolmenwood-core-books-bundle?affiliate_id=1070389) campaign. I always planned to write this post but I'm finally getting around to it because she wrote a [post of her own](https://patchworkpaladin.com/2026/05/12/a-dolmenwood-retrospective/) and I don't want to read it until I have got my thoughts down. Check it out, it will no doubt be excellent. 

![Gnarled tree roots forming a face in a dark wood, with two glowing fairies hovering nearby. Dolmenwood interior art.](</published_assets/Dolmenwood_Player_s_Book_2024-03-05 2.webp>)

The campaign was wonderful, both in the sense of good and in the sense of being filled with wonder. The GM and players are all excellent and we could have had fun playing just about anything but the GM was clearly at home in this whimsy filled wonderland and if I was to describe the setting I most wanted to play in it would be a fairytale forest with whimsy and a little horror, so it was always going to be a good fit. 

We played three arcs with three parties over 72 sessions in just under two years and so got to explore a great deal of the different things the setting had to offer. 

# Arc One - Prigwort to the Abbey 
For the first 41 sessions I played Tamrin, a funny looking fellow: excessively hairy with a Misshapen skull and droll lupine mouth. Rather miserly as demonstrated by his clothes being hessian rags. As a hobby he collects saintly relics and believes the dead are rising but he is sure his faith will protect him. 

Most of the detail came from the excellent random tables in the character generation section. We did the standard 3d6 down the line then chose Kindred and Class (Human Cleric). I like this a lot, you don't have to feel bound to the rolls you make but there is enough there along with a random name table to make your character feel like they fit the setting and give enough prompts that you can figure out who they are as you play without having a heavy background to worry about. They also work well for the GM who can use them to easily make interesting NPCs with a few built-in quirks. 

We started with the introductory adventure, The Pipes of Droomen Knoll. Searching for a missing child, be careful though, strange noises have been heard and there are Crookhorns about!  
It was a fun little adventure that got us nicely into the system and used to dungeon turns and slow movement while introducing three of the factions. 
I didn't read any of the books while we were playing except the Player's Book but looking at it now the layout for the adventures is fantastic. Great use of formatting and bolded text to make it very table-usable. 

We returned in triumph, though my character did contract pubic lice from searching the filthy crookhorn's bedding (it's true, why do you all look so sceptical?)

![Page from the Dolmenwood adventure appendix showing the table-usable format: numbered area entries with bolded keywords and parenthetical detail.](</published_assets/Dolmenwood Adventures.webp>)

After Droomen Knoll we followed some of the plentiful hooks (if anything there was too much going on, having to prioritise our aims felt a little weird being more used to more linear WotC style adventures), we cleared some shrines then went in search of a missing girl, Violet Harrowmoor, daughter of one of the Noble Houses. We tracked her to the [Ruined Abbey of St. Clewydd](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/527291/the-ruined-abbey-of-st-clewyd?affiliate_id=1070389), another absolutely wonderful dungeon corrupted by the Nag-Lord's influence. It had plenty to do, with ghostly monks, monsters to fight and puzzles to solve. The location and layout were top-class and the adventure had real pathos. 
We are quite a slow group and spent many sessions here gradually exploring the place, clearing the outbuildings one-by-one before heading down into the dungeon proper. 

By the time we were finished we were level 5 and dripping with magic items. We decided to take a hiatus and switch to a different party to explore some more of the wood. 

# Interlude - The Encounters
While exploring the woods we ran into several random encounters, these were as rich and varied as everything else in the setting, particularly memorable were the sentient plants (don't eat them you go mad), holy acolytes (assholes), the wild hunt (terrifying), cobbin (awakened animal) refugees (cute and touching). 
The campaign book has great encounter tables split by time of day and with sub-tables for common, rare, animal and regional encounters to give each area a different feel alongside tables for what your encounter is doing. 

# Arc Two - Death in Lankshorn
As a change from our previous characters we played a more ragged bunch, hired by our previous characters to clear out an old tower. I played a Grimalkin (anthropomorphic cat) thief, Jack Flippancy. Jack's former career was as a stage magician and he arrived in the Dolmenwood from fairy after messing up a magic trick and pulling himself out of a hat. Making the best of it he stayed here to fulfil his life's dream of growing enormously fat. (More detail sparked from the random character rolls.)
![Three Grimalkin cat-folk adventurers in a mossy forest — an armoured warrior with sword, a staff-bearing companion, and a map-reading mage — attended by fairies.](</published_assets/Dolmenwood_Player_s_Book_2024-03-05 3.webp>)
I was sad to leave Tamrin behind until I started playing Jack; he was great fun, helped enormously by my appetite for whimsy and wordplay meshing so well with the setting and our GM. 
Grimalkin have fairy glamours, minor magical abilities. Jack rolled silver tongue, the ability to speak to animals. We went full fairytale and decided this only worked if all the speech was in rhyming couplets. 
Literally fantastic. 

We started in a new town, Lankshorn, in the south where there were political shenanigans going on between noble rivals and we dropped into another of the pre-written adventures, [Emelda's Song](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/527293/emelda-s-song?affiliate_id=1070389). Our investigations led us to the Malbleat family crypts where we encountered strange experiments, cursed creatures, the bad kind of fairy wine and, thanks to our foolish overconfidence, our Doom.
![Red-haired fairies crushed inside a stone mortar and pestle, red juice running down its side — the bad kind of fairy wine.](</published_assets/Fairy Pestle.webp>)
After just 8 sessions this arc ended in defeat as we tried to prevent Lord Malbleat's vile ritual and decided to take on the undead head-on rather than trying for a smart solution. 
This is still a deadly system, especially at lower levels. We were all sad to lose our characters; they were a weird bunch but they had great chemistry. 

# Interlude - Magic and Religion
The magic system packs in more of the delicious Dolmenwood flavour, magic users (magicians) get the worst of it, there is nothing bad here but they get fairly ordinary spells learned in the normal way. Enchanters and Clerics (and Friars, less fighty clerics with magician spell-progression) are lavished with flavourful magic systems. 

Enchanters have a system of Fairy Magic consisting of Glamours (the same small abilities that the fairy races get, though they gain one every level, fairies just get one), and Fairy Runes (which can vary greatly in power and are granted randomly). 
Lesser, Greater and Mighty Runes have variable uses by level but early on can be used once daily, once per level or just once. Our enchanter at level 3 gained the Mighty Rune of Death: 4d8 levels of mortals must save versus doom or die. Insane power, but usable once ever until level 10 when it can be used once a year. 

Clerics and Friars gain holy magic, essentially the standard selection of cleric spells but each one named for a Saint of the Pluritine Church, with a little blurb about what the saint is known for.  The holy spellcasters we had in our game made great use of this to add immersion and richness to the world. We both made up impromptu prayers of intercession with the appropriate saints as we cast. 

![Dolmenwood spell entry for Bless, here named 'Righteousness of St Gondyw', with a flavour sidebar describing the saint's miracle.](</published_assets/Bless.webp>)
The Pluritine Church, the organised religion of the One True God, is one of the factions within Dolmenwood and an easy supplier of plot-hooks as they wish to restore the many ruined shrines to be found in the wood. There are other religions but the Catholic-coded Pluritine Church is the dominant one, as an atheist with some Catholic education it worked very well for me but I know some people do find it off-putting.   
# Arc Three - Fungus, Fairyland and Finale
We rolled another set of characters, this time I got Rogbert Sundeman, a vain, arrogant and cowardly Human Knight. After our last party died opposing House Malbleat's wicked plans, I of course chose to swear my fealty to House Malbleat. 
Our GM bounced back from her first TPK with great style and rolled the results of our failing to stop the ritual into our next arc as we embarked on a quest to find a cure for Lord Malbleat, struck down by the thing he had sought to control and which had escaped into the forest.   
I had a great time here, repeatedly spending all of the party's money on fine clothes, expensive wine and horses. Which makes me sound like a dick but we were a very high-trust table by this point, the players were cool with it even if the characters might not have loved it. 
![Arthur Rackham-style watercolour of an armoured knight driving his sword into a fanged dragon while villagers watch from doorways.](</published_assets/Sir Rogbert.webp>)
We started with (I believe) [The Fungus that Came to Blackeswell](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/527289/the-fungus-that-came-to-blackeswell?affiliate_id=1070389) though we soon went completely off track. I think at least one of the other players was a little frustrated with us starting over at first level again and they really wanted to get more involved with faction play so we decided to shake things up a little. 
Abandoning Lord Malbleat to his fate (the danger of being a chaotic aligned lord sending out chaotic knights), we did various shenanigans, dropped a village into the fairy-realm then scarpered down a fairy road to avoid pursuit. We spent quite a few sessions here playing through [The Labyrinth](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/320014/jim-henson-s-labyrinth-the-adventure-game?affiliate_id=1070389&keyword=labyrinth) which was a fantastic fit here. 
Sir Rogbert became the only knight in the Dolmenwood knighted by a true king, before returning to a randomly determined hex to rumours of a new power in the woods raising a great black tower (surely nothing to do with us). 

By this point we had achieved enough familiarity and confidence with the system that we were freely mixing in races and classes from OSE or homebrew, and it took me an embarrassing number of sessions (and a Ben Milton video where he showed some interior art) to realise that we were playing something that wasn't even written for the same ruleset, much less that it wasn't Dolmenwood material. 

We had more fun investigating a village stuck in time and trying to win the hand of a Fair Lady Sorceress, but family and work commitments combined with the pressure of our game slot being a timezone compromise that wasn't ideal for anyone meant we decided to bring things to a close. 

As sad a day as I have had playing a TTRPG, I expect to remember this game for the rest of my life and will be surprised and elated if I ever play such a good game again. 

# Interlude - Eat, Drink and be Merry
![A Dolmenwood feast: piled fruits, breads, cheeses, and a bone-filled tureen between flaming candelabras, in warm yellows and browns.](</published_assets/Food and drink.webp>)
There is so much richness in the material, I have still only skimmed the Campaign Book which I know has much more than is in the Player's Book but the local foods, wines and other drinks, pipe leaf (and the "give us a hint GM" smoke and ponder mechanic) along with the character generation, the magic, the setting information and more all combine into a rich stew that could only be surpassed perhaps by Sir Rogbert's favourite Larks tongues in Aspic, or Tamrin's delight with the more homely Trottel Mash. 

# The System
Dolmenwood is from the makers of [Old-School Essentials](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/publisher/5606/necrotic-gnome/category/32434/old-school-essentials?affiliate_id=1070389), itself a close copy of the original [Moldvay Basic/Expert D&D](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/110274/d-d-basic-set-rulebook-b-x-ed-basic?affiliate_id=1070389). It is very close to OSE with just a few tweaks. 

Thief skills are d6 rolls to hit a target number that decreases with level, which I much prefer over the usual percentile system. Ability checks are d6 + ability modifier succeeding on a 4+. It seemed odd at first but the probability comes out pretty close to d20 roll-under checks and I liked it. 
The biggest issue was that both ability and skill checks are d6 roll high but one succeeds on a set number and adds a modifier while one has a variable target number. Because it was an OSR game with few checks it took a while to get the hang of which was which. 

Saving throws are the same as OSE but renamed to a slightly more user-friendly doom/ray/hold/blast/spell rather than death/wands/paralysis/breath/spell. 

You are covered whether you prefer separate race and class or race-as-class as Dolmenwood gives both options. 

It also allows for slot-based encumbrance or coin-weight, we went with slot-based, but as we tracked it on a Google spreadsheet I think weight would have been easier, slots start to break down a little with things like pack animals and large numbers of rations.   

There isn't much more to say, there are Dolmenwood specific races and classes but it is all minor tweaks to the 1981 version of D&D that folks have been playing for 45 years. I won't argue about which is the best version of D&D because we all know in our hearts it is this one. 

# Conclusion
I don't know if this is the best TTRPG ever made, but I think it might be the best for me. I love the setting to the point that even St. Willowfrith, patron of pie-makers and repentance, might consider it sinful. 

If I have to pick fault, and I guess I should try for at least a little balance in a review then I would say it might be a little too rich, I don't remember the final count for the number of hexes we crawled but it was maybe in the low double-digits. Everything is so packed with detail that you can spend weeks in a single hex. In play it felt almost fractal, as we zoomed in on any detail it expanded out before us, we never seemed to find an end of interesting things to do, I know that can't all be the book and that our excellent GM must have contributed a lot, but from conversation with her and looking at the book myself there is plenty there to support that. 
It did leave us a little disoriented in our first campaign as players mostly coming from more linear games. When we started over we had seven explicit "quests" we could pursue and two-dozen rumours. It took a while to realise we didn't have to do everything, we could just do what seemed most important, urgent, or even just the most interesting and learn to let the others go. 

The other aspect of its richness might also be that it does have a strong flavour, if it is a flavour you enjoy that is of course excellent news, but I can imagine someone who didn't like it would find it too much. Certainly the criticism of it that I have seen online is centred around the plethora of races and the heavy religious content. 

If you like strongly grounded fantasy with a majority-human setting or hate any whiff of real-world religion in your game you will feel pretty lost in these woods. That's OK, not everyone loves every flavour but if you love fairytales full of whimsy but also some cruelty and darkness then come into the woods with me. 

I promise you will have a good time. 
You may get lost here but who would ever want to leave anyway?
