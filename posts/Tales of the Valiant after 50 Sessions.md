![Tales of the Valiant Logo](https://www.talesofthevaliant.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/Tales-of-the-Valiant-Logo-STANDARD-FINAL-1024x329.png)In January of 2025, I started a campaign running [Tales of the Valiant](https://www.talesofthevaliant.com/) (ToV). We have now run fifty sessions, so I have a little bit more to go on than my initial impressions from reading through the book. I'll cover what is in there, how I have found it at the table and consider whether I think it is worth buying.  
# Introduction 
After 2023's OGL fiasco, where Wizards of the Coast tried to revoke their relatively open D&D license, several companies embarked on projects to make games of their own. The one most obviously similar to Dungeons & Dragons 5th edition was Kobold Press's Tales of the Valiant, initially called [Black Flag](https://koboldpress.com/black-flag-roleplaying/), which is still the name of their open rule set for it. 
They ran a Kickstarter which raised $1.15 million dollars, and in mid-2024, duly delivered it. 

# Tales of the Valiant
ToV follows the D&D tradition of three core books, a Players Guide (PG), Monster Vault (MV) and Game Masters Guide (GMG). The main change in terms of content is that the Player's Guide contains the magic item descriptions meaning that the Game Master's Guide is less necessary for play. It is largely compatible with D&D 5e14 and 5e24 as well as third party supplements for them. 
## Player's Guide
This contains the bulk of the rules needed to make a character and play the game. 
I like the character rules, subclasses are a little limited at two per class but there is still good variety. 
Kobold Press split "race" into lineage and heritage which is a nice way to allow players to either lean into fantasy archetypes or play against them. You pick ability scores once to get numbers that match D&D's but sidestep exactly what contributes to them and you don't need to pick them then work out where the bonuses go. Elegant design. 
Talents replace feats and they are split into magic, martial and technical with classes being able to choose from the one relevant to them. Players take appropriate feats that aren't tied to ability scores and your fighters don't suddenly gain the ability to cast spells from nowhere. 
## Monster Vault
The monsters are good, the statblocks are well thought out doing away with ability scores in favour of bonuses that makes them easy to read at the table. They hit appropriately hard and generally their actions make them effective and their bonus action does something flavourful but that isn't the end of the world if you forget in a busy combat. The art is good too but the layout is rough in places, with some monsters being split across pages in an annoying way. 
## Game Masters Guide
The best 5e Game Masters Guide by far, good advice, good variant and extra rules, a solid section on homebrewing, random tables and campaign dressing that are table-useful. I think there is so much DM advice freely available on the internet that a GMG is not necessary and ToV is structured in a way that you have everything you need to play without one, but if you want one, this is the one. 
# Playing the Game
I have run 50 sessions now and the characters are just about to hit level 10 and the system has been great. We are playing [Dungeons of Drakkenheim](https://ghostfiregaming.com/dungeons-of-drakkenheim/), a dark fantasy sandbox campaign by the [Dungeon Dudes](https://www.youtube.com/@DungeonDudes) that takes place in a ruined city, in my opinion the best 5e campaign out there.  The characters feel very balanced, they are all very different but in combat they all pull their weight and they all have plenty to do outside of combat. 
Highlights from my side have really been around the players and the growth and interactions of their characters, which is a good thing. The system has pretty much got out of the way and let us play but when I have noticed it it has generally been good. I have run lots of 5e both 2014 and 2024 versions and in ToV I get a lot less of those wince-inducing moments where players do something within the rules that leaves me thinking "I hate that". 
## The Good
They have taken the 5e14 rules and filed off the worst of the rough edges, there are fewer must-have talents (think great weapon master or sharpshooter) or dud subclasses. They have added weapon masteries to make martials a little more interesting but unless you are a fighter you have to choose between the damage and the mastery effect which is a nice way to do it. They have made some nice changes to ritual magic, rituals are now separate from your normal spells, they don't need spell slots and can't be upcast but many do upscale with your level so they remain relevant. 
I love the luck mechanic (once per turn when you fail a save or an attack roll gain one luck. Spend one luck to add 1 to a d20 roll or three for a re-roll), as GM I rarely have the bandwidth to be thinking about inspiration and giving a little consolation for a miss is nice. I have been using it in my 5e games since the first Black Flag playtest document dropped. It is all player managed and if they don't remember it then it isn't my problem. 
As we reach level 10 combat is slowing down a little, it is a general 5e problem as players get so many features and options but it is better than my 2024 D&D game and I am still able to challenge the players effectively with just the creatures in the Monster Vault. 
Kobold Press continues to support ToV with additional core books (Player's Guide 2 and Monster Vault 2) as well as settings and short adventures. There is not yet the same volume of material as Dungeons and Dragons 5e with its 12-year history, but there is quite a lot available now. There are 2 rule expansions, 16 short adventures, several settings with accompanying adventure books both published and in the works (there are enough that getting an exact count was a pain). If you like their settings you could play a lot of games without ever leaving their ecosystem. 
Another benefit over D&D is that the rules are available as a PDF, rather than leasing them on an online platform like D&D Beyond you can download them and have a copy you control. 
## The Bad
The only thing I have really noticed is an interaction between two of the talents. Lucky, which grants two luck when you would get one and Comrade, which allows you to spend your luck to aid an ally when they spend a luck of their own. Two of my players spotted this as a powerful combination. I don't really mind the power it adds but it makes every roll that isn't an obvious failure or success into an exercise in accountancy as they ask if the other player has luck and decide if and how many extra luck they would add. It takes a great mechanic and turns it into a pain. I think they are fine separately but terrible as a combo, I would houserule one or the other if I run ToV again.    
## The Other
As we are playing Dungeons of Drakkenheim there are classes and subclasses available that are written for 5e14. They don't quite fit with the ToV rules, mainly the ToV classes all having their subclasses at level 3, but there are a few other bits like Eldritch Blast being a class feature rather than a spell. It isn't anything that can't be easily dealt with but I did need to make some ruling on how things worked. 
## Online Play 
Online play is not as well supported as either version of D&D 5e. I play on Foundry and while the Player's and Monster books are well implemented adding the Drakkenheim 5e modules has been a real pain point. There is a converter but you have to load the module in a 5e game, export what you want with the Black Flag conversion module then import it into your ToV game. It is finicky and doesn't always work. I had to write a python script to fix all the issues when I added the Monsters of Drakkenheim. On Foundry there are far fewer ToV compatible modules than 5e ones which is a shame because there are some I really miss. 
It is perfectly playable and some things like the luck mechanic and character builder are very nicely implemented, but I miss the ease of grabbing any 5e monster and dropping it into my game which is very simple with D&D 5e. 
# Conclusion
I have played both the 2014 and 2025 versions of D&D 5e and Tales of the Valiant is better than either of them, though they are all similar enough in truth that you can mix all three at the table without real issues. 
If you are new to 5e buying Tales of the Valiant will get you the best game, but if you aren't new to it I don't know if it is worth the cost to switch, you could just add a few things from the Black Flag SRD as house rules and call it good. 
If you are a player transitioning to Game Master then I would certainly recommend the ToV GMG over either 5e version, it lacks magic items but there are plenty freely available in the 5e SRD and I like to make custom ones anyway.
If you are playing online in a full-featured VTT like Foundry then unless you plan to only use Kobold Press material I would stick with D&D just for the compatibility with third party modules. If you use a light VTT like Owlbear Rodeo then ToV is the best option.
