I love [Daggerheart](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/browse?affiliate_id=1070389&keyword=daggerheart), I wrote a [[What I Talk About When I Talk About Daggerheart|review about why]]. The book is pretty, the system is good and it encourages a play style I enjoy. High-trust collaborative gaming is my jam. 

One of the criticisms Daggerheart gets (others are available, it's an opinionated system that isn't for everyone) is that there isn't any first-party adventure content for it. I thought the same at first but now I think I was wrong. 

# What Is Available?
The core book has six mini-settings with an innovative campaign frame system. They have a campaign pitch, general principles, special mechanics for the frame, etc. They are good, and rightly got a lot of praise when they were first released. But the only adventure content there is an inciting incident, a few short paragraphs providing a hook for the first session. 

The one adventure, Sablewood Messengers, has been available since beta testing and has a 10-page setting to go with it, and it's a single-session quickstart adventure. 
That's it for first-party setting or adventure content after the first year except for a convention adventure not readily available. 

The Hope and Fear rules supplement is due soon but is mostly mechanics and more campaign frames. There is another one-shot adventure for Free RPG Day, and an announced Chris Perkins adventure supplement, details and release date pending. 

Some major third-party releases have been announced, a Dungeons of Drakkenheim conversion and something from Eberron creator Keith Baker, both announced by Darrington Press and are if not official then have their blessing.  

# Is This Enough? 
I was in the definite no camp. When Daggerheart released I wanted an official adventure to show me how it's supposed to work. 

I've changed my mind.

I have played in two campaigns and several one-shots and I don't think they would have been better if they had released either a full campaign setting like Tal'dorei or a full WotC-style campaign adventure. 

The longer campaign had a setting based on starting with a map and going through the steps in the core book to set up expectations around Content, Aim, and Tone for the game. The shorter one that is still ongoing is a highly creative framework based on time travel, the [Bureau of Chronological Affairs](https://dngnclub.itch.io/bureau-of-chronological-affairs). 

If there had been an official setting or adventure then it's likely we would have played that. I can see that there is value to a shared experience, and in some ways it would have been good to have all the Daggerheart fans having shared that experience. 
I do not think it would have been good for the game. With no set adventure path in front of us we were free to explore the map (that looks like a big skull, let's check it out, is that thing in the desert a spa? we should go). We would not have done that if we knew there was a path to follow. Our GM was stretched by having to come up with the content himself but I think he would agree it helped him grow his skills.

But the real benefit is the homegrown tradition. 
# It's the Culture Innit
When the original D&D released in 1974, there was no adventure material until a few pages in Blackmoor a year later. Then there were a couple of modules distributed but not written by TSR in 1976-1977 (analogous to our Drakkenheim/Keith Baker Daggerheart supplements perhaps). TSR published the first in-house written modules 4 years after D&D's release in 1978.

Gary and Dave captured something special when they wrote D&D, but from what I have read of the games Gary ran I think the hobby benefited from there not being a fully written out Castle Greyhawk dungeon to play in. I think having him take a bigger role in showing us what to do would have been to the detriment of the hobby. 

Matters of taste aside, would we have got the Caverns of Thracia or City State of the Invincible Overlord if there had been a glut of official content? I think the lack of initial adventures actually helped D&D thrive because if you wanted to play you had to learn to be a game designer. D&D became a creative and collaborative folk tradition that we have benefited from ever since. 

I think we are seeing some of that replicated with Daggerheart. 

I love the fact that the community has stepped up and made things itself with no authoritative voice to show us the "one true way".
There are [nearly 500 Daggerheart supplements on DTRPG at time of writing.](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/browse?ruleSystem=1000562-daggerheart&src=fid1000562&page=1&sortBy=rating&affiliate_id=1070389) The quality is variable, but the standard seems generally high, and some look very professional. 

Would it have happened if there was an official setting? 

Probably, but I know that if I write an adventure or supplement one of the first things I do is to look at the official releases to "see how it is done", to take elements of their style and look at what is included so I can make sure my work fits the same mould. 

I was thinking of making a campaign frame earlier and my first port-of-call was to an official one to make sure I had the same headings and "did it properly". 
When I wrote [[Jumping on the Quantum Railroad|my monster supplement]] there was nothing official to compare it to, I just wrote it, my style, my voice. When there is more first-party content available will that mean there is more structure there for us to quietly copy, more of a box we have to make effort to think outside? While there is a lack of official content we don't know what the box is supposed to look like and the constraints are the ones we make ourselves.

The other great thing about the lack of first-party content is that I'm actually looking seriously at the third-party products. I ran ten published campaign adventures in 5e and despite having just as many third-party books on my shelves as WotC ones, nine of them were from WotC.  
# Long May it Continue
While this time last year I would have torn your arm off for a chance at a first-party supplement, I saw Matt Mercer, Jeremy Crawford and Chris Perkins' work when I played 5e. They have undeniable talent, but after playing a more collaborative game in Daggerheart I have realised that what they were making wasn't for me. I'll be interested to see their contributions to Daggerheart but if a linear campaign adventure was for me then I'd be planning my next campaign in 5.5e, not Daggerheart.

I have preordered Hope and Fear and I'm looking forward to it. I backed the Daggerheart Drakkenheim Kickstarter and I'll enjoy seeing how they converted it. I might even run it one day, but I'm no longer hungry for it. 
The wait for official content may not be over, but I no longer care. 

Can I make a campaign as polished as Darrington Press will? 

No chance. 

Can I make a campaign my players will enjoy as much as a Darrington Press one? 

I'll give it a damn good shot, and I'll come out of it a better GM than I would running another official campaign adventure.
