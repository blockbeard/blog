![[Jim_Henson_and_the_Muppets.webp]]I came across this in a patreon post from [Iris Compiet](https://www.patreon.com/posts/so-many-feelings-152679083), an artist I follow, talking about a discussion she had moderated.
 
> There is a story about a young boy meeting Jim Henson at an exhibition for The Dark Crystal. Urged by his mother to ask a question, the boy looked around at the creatures, the costumes, the fantastic world and asked, "How did you make all of this?" Henson didn't talk about glue or fabric. Instead, he told him:
> 
> "you surround yourself with talented people you love, and who love you, give them an idea with enough empty space to invite them in and make it their own... and when it returns to you, it's more beautiful than you imagined."

It seems to originate from [this TikTok by Alexander Polinsky](https://www.tiktok.com/@alexanderpolinsky/video/7294118753527237934).

I'm not much of an artist, and what art I do make is almost always a solo project, even when it probably should be a collaboration. But of course there is some art I make and participate in that I rarely think of as "art". When I read it, my mind immediately went to the Tabletop RPGs I play. 
When I think of the games that have been the best and the games that have been the worst, this quote explains almost all of the differences between them.

I doubt I'm telling anyone sufficiently into TTRPGs to be reading my blog anything they don't already know, but perhaps it is good to have a reminder to do this with intention rather than to happen upon it by chance. 
# How can we do that?
Breaking it down, there are five main parts to this.
- talent
- love
- ideas
- space
- invitation
## Talent
This may be one of the hardest parts. Everyone wants good players, but how do you get them?
This could easily be its own post on how to find players, there is plenty of advice about that already so let's instead think about what talent is in this context. 

I certainly don't think it is talent in the way that the actual play scene might make you think. You don't need to be a consummate actor, you don't need perfect voices, you don't need to have mastered the system, and I don't think you have to be incredible at improv either. 
I think talent in this context is largely being open to the rest of the proposition, and allowing the other players to be open to it. 
I would take a new player who was willing to take risks, be creative and cheer on the other players over an expert player who was a professional actor, and could do their character's voice well enough to work for Disney but who didn't trust the other players or give them space to develop their own ideas.
## ~~Love~~ Trust
Well, first of all, let's change it up a little. I'm English and socially awkward, so let's get rid of that "love". 
I was going to send the quote that inspired this post to a couple of my GMs but in order to maintain a proper facade, I would have to caveat that "love" in so many ways, as a friend, not in a creepy way, that it just becomes a mess of excuses and mitigation rather than a genuine compliment. 

So let's call it trust, as I think that is the heart of the matter. Love can come later. 
Or not, as you prefer. 

Trust is a hard thing to achieve and an easy thing to lose. 
The only way to see if you can trust someone is to put yourself in a position where you are vulnerable and see whether they take advantage of that. It's scary, but if you do it a few times and they don't betray you, then you can trust them more. 

But how does this work in TTRPGs?

Playing without guardrails and not pushing people off the cliff. 

This can be hard, players might be hesitant to just ask for what they want, afraid of a refusal. As a GM you might be hesitant to say yes, afraid of setting a precedent or not knowing how it will affect your game. 
Some of this might be wanting to adhere to the rules and "do it properly". Some might be because of having been burned in the past, or being worried about being burned now. The internet is full of stories of rules-lawyer players breaking games with nonsense and controlling GMs giving the players no freedom. 
You can't just let anyone do whatever they want without any regard for rules or consequences, but if you tell a player a hard no because you are worried about the game, you will train them not to ask. The short term relief of not having to worry about it, or them bypassing something you planned is likely to lead to a worse game in the end. 

This goes both ways, be trustworthy. As a player don't try and push for an unreasonable advantage, as a GM don't slap down your players for asking. OK, so they used a cunning trick to kill the BBEG. They got to feel smart and now someone will step into the power vacuum and the game heads in a new direction.

When you have a group who can trust each other to not try and "win the game", then we all win the game. 

## Ideas
Come to the table with ideas, not a finished product. 

A finished story you will narrate to your players is not an idea. 
A detailed player backstory explaining every aspect of their personality, and a scheme to achieve some goal with all of the steps laid out is not an idea. 

Ideas have room to breathe, they are unfinished and flexible. 
You can prepare your game, you can decide you are playing in the city of Drakkenheim and that the game will take place there without wandering off to explore the wider world, you can have a map with locations that have certain things in them, these are all ideas that can flourish into something that is built together at the table. 
What isn't an idea is scripting it. First they will go to the village and meet the Hooded Lanterns, then they will head to the Rat's Nest and fight the Rat Prince and then they will level up and then they will...    

If a game is a tree, then ideas are seeds. You know an acorn will grow into an oak tree, but you don't know how many branches it will have, what birds will roost there or even if it will grow to sprout acorns of its own. That is what we find out at the table.  
## Space
As a GM, you will inevitably have to do some level of preparation, even if you use random tables and improv more than planned encounters, but you should hold your preparation lightly, give the session space to grow, however unexpected the direction it takes. The most fun and memorable sessions are often the ones that don't follow the "plan". 

Give the players space to express their characters, don't default to no if they want to do something that isn't strictly by the rules, whether it is using a fireball to light the campfire or playing a background that isn't in the book. 
Think about how you can help them make it work, and don't punish them for it (there is that trust again).
## Invitation
Let your players have agency, the basic level is just not being a dick and railroading them about, but a good GM goes further than just describing the scene and asking "what do you do?" 
If they want to buy new armour, ask them what it looks like, what device do they want painted on the shield? If they want to look for an NPC to help, ask how they know about them.

Coming back to trust, follow through with the invitation. If they ask to have a device painted on their shield or to customise their armour to have spikes, go with it, don't then make it expensive or difficult. When they do take your invitation and make something different honour that intention and have it mean something. It doesn't have to be something big, but bring it up, have someone ask about their device, have them look askance at the spiky armour, make the players feel like the things you invite them to contribute are meaningful. 

# Something Beautiful Returns
I think the best GMs do this regardless of the system they run, and none of this is really mechanical. 
I think of myself as a pretty great player and a mediocre GM, and I think all GMs would do well to take Jim Henson's advice because it is the difference I see as a player between the mediocre and the great games that I play.

You should try to find ways to bring trust and openness to all of your games, whether you are the GM or a player, and whether it is a crunchy power fantasy or gritty survival game. 

If you can pull it off, then everyone wins.
